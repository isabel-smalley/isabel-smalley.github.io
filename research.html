Flora of Glensheen

During the spring and summer of 2022, I had the invaluable opportunity to contribute to a community-driven research project spearheaded by Dr. Amanda Grusz. This pioneering initiative centered around the exploration and documentation of plant biodiversity at a local UMD-owned site, Glensheen Manor. Community members enthusiastically engaged in the collection of plant specimens from this picturesque locale. As an active participant in this endeavor, I played a vital role in the subsequent phases of the project. My responsibilities primarily revolved around sequencing and mounting the collected specimens. Working alongside fellow undergraduates and dedicated community members, I meticulously processed numerous plant samples, ensuring accurate identification and meticulous preservation. My involvement extended beyond mere data collection; it represented a meaningful collaboration between academia and the community. By contributing my skills to the Grusz Lab and Olga Lakela Herbarium, I not only gained invaluable hands-on experience in plant biology but also actively contributed to the expansion of scientific knowledge and the preservation of botanical heritage. This immersive research experience profoundly influenced my academic journey, igniting a passion for community-engaged research and reinforcing my commitment to advancing our understanding of the natural world.

Myriopteris Genus

As a dedicated participant in a pioneering research endeavor led by Dr. Amanda Grusz, I embarked on a journey to unravel the intricate mysteries of the desert-adapted fern genus Myriopteris. Alongside my esteemed peers, Nick Weiss and Ernie Forgach, I devoted countless hours to this ambitious project, emerging as the driving force behind its success. Our overarching objective was ambitious yet clear: to illuminate the phylogenetic relationships, unveil cryptic diversity, and elucidate ploidy levels within the enigmatic genus Myriopteris. This formidable task required meticulous attention to detail and unwavering dedication, qualities that I was proud to exemplify throughout the project's duration. Our methodology was comprehensive and multifaceted. We meticulously examined herbarium specimens spanning the entirety of the Myriopteris genus, meticulously measuring spore diameters and meticulously counting spores per sporangium. Leveraging cutting-edge GoFlag sequencing techniques and gametophyte culturing methodologies, we delved deep into the genetic underpinnings of these resilient ferns. From the fall of 2022 to the fall of 2023, I immersed myself wholeheartedly in this monumental undertaking. Single-handedly, I meticulously scrutinized 106 herbarium specimens and meticulously extracted DNA from over 50, ensuring the integrity and accuracy of our data. My unwavering commitment and exceptional contributions to the project did not go unnoticed. I was honored to receive the Botanical Society of America Undergraduate Research Award, a testament to the significance and impact of our collective efforts. This recognition serves as a poignant reminder of the transformative power of scientific inquiry and the boundless possibilities that await those who dare to explore the unknown.

Myriopteris covillei

Under the mentorship of Dr. Amanda Grusz, I embarked on a captivating research journey exploring cryptic diversity within the species Myriopteris covillei. From the autumn of 2022 to the present day, I dedicated myself wholeheartedly to unraveling the intricacies of this enigmatic fern species, employing a multidimensional approach that combined morphological analysis, spore morphology, geographic distribution, and molecular data. As the sole undergraduate contributor to this groundbreaking project, I embraced the challenges and opportunities with unwavering determination. With meticulous attention to detail, I meticulously examined morphological traits, meticulously measured spore diameters, and meticulously counted spores per sporangium, all while meticulously scrutinizing the species' geographic range for subtle variations. Leveraging cutting-edge molecular techniques, I delved deep into the genetic makeup of Myriopteris covillei, uncovering hidden patterns and revealing previously unrecognized cryptic diversity. My findings thus far have been both compelling and illuminating, underscoring the importance of a holistic approach to species delineation and conservation. Furthermore, my dedication to botanical research extends beyond the laboratory. I am currently participating in BURST, a prestigious summer research opportunity for undergraduates offered through UMD. Through this program, I am continuing to delve into the complexities of Myriopteris covillei and its cryptic counterparts, building upon the foundation laid during my earlier research endeavors. In May, I had the privilege of embarking on a field expedition to the Sonoran and Mojave deserts, where I conducted firsthand observations and collected specimens of Myriopteris covillei and their cryptic counterparts. This immersive fieldwork experience provided invaluable insights and enriched my understanding of desert ecosystems, further fueling my passion for botanical exploration. As I continue to explore the cryptic diversity of Myriopteris covillei, I am excited to uncover new discoveries and contribute to our understanding of plant evolution and biodiversity. I eagerly anticipate the opportunity to share our findings and insights with the scientific community and inspire future generations of botanists.

Hornworts & Dioicy

During the summer of 2023, I had the privilege of participating in cutting-edge research through the prestigious NSF REU program. Under the guidance of esteemed scientists Dr. Peter Schafran and Dr. Fay-Wei Li, I embarked on an exciting investigation into the genetic underpinnings of sex determination in hornworts. Nestled within the vibrant research community of the Boyce Thompson Institute on the Cornell campus, our project focused on unraveling the complexities of sex chromosomes in three hornwort species. Specifically, we sought to elucidate the genetic mechanisms governing the transition from monoicy to dioicy within this evolutionary clade. Drawing upon a multidisciplinary approach encompassing genomics, molecular biology, and bioinformatics, I immersed myself in the intricacies of our research endeavor. From DNA sequencing and genome assembly to comparative genomic analysis, every step of the process was a testament to our collective dedication and scientific curiosity. Working alongside a team of talented researchers, I marveled at the power of collaborative inquiry and the boundless potential of scientific exploration. Together, we delved deep into the genetic landscapes of hornwort genomes, uncovering hidden patterns and unlocking new insights into the fascinating world of plant sex determination. My experience with the NSF REU program was transformative, both personally and professionally. It offered me a unique opportunity to engage in hands-on research at the forefront of botanical science while fostering invaluable connections within the scientific community. As I reflect on my time at the Boyce Thompson Institute, I am filled with gratitude for the mentorship of Dr. Schafran and Dr. Li, whose guidance and expertise enriched my research experience and inspired me to pursue further studies in plant biology. I am thrilled to have the opportunity to present our findings at the upcoming Botany conference in June, where I look forward to sharing our insights and engaging with fellow scholars and enthusiasts alike. This conference presentation represents a culmination of our collective efforts and a testament to the exciting discoveries that await in the field of evolutionary biology.
