Flora of Glensheen

During the spring and summer of 2022, I had the invaluable opportunity to contribute to a community-driven research project spearheaded by Dr. Amanda Grusz. This project centered around citizen and undergrad documentation of plant biodiversity at Duluth’s historic university-owned Glensheen Manor. Community members and undergraduates were encouraged to come and learn about plant collection and identification from scientists at UMD by participating in collecting specimens from Glensheen’s grounds. My role came in the subsequent phases of this project which involved sequencing and mounting the community collected specimens along with my fellow undergraduate peers. I was happy to have one of my first research projects be a collaboration between academia and community and to be involved with preserving botanical heritage. This research experience changed by academic trajectory, instilling a passion for research, and teaching me about how to integrate science and community.

Myriopteris Genus

Cheilanthoid ferns (Pteridaceae) are a xeric-adapted, monophyletic clade with a propensity for hybridization and whole genome duplication. Cheilanthoids have extensive morphological convergence due to the arid environments in which they live (Tryon and Tryon 1973, Rothfels et al. 2008). Historically, the clade has been grouped under the genus Cheilanthes Sw. However, molecular phylogenetic analyses revealed the genus to be polyphyletic, and the need to define new monophyletic genera arose (Rothfels et al. 2008; Windham et al. 2009; Eiserhardt et al. 2011). One such genus is Myriopteris Fée, a monophyletic clade of species that reside mostly in North and Central America, with some taxa extending to southern South America and Africa (Grusz and Windham 2013). Myriopteris includes many lineages that undergo obligate apomixis, but little is known about the evolution of the trait and its relationship to abiotic environment. Recently, Grusz et al. (2021) proposed that arid environments are particularly well-suited for the evolution of obligate apomixis in ferns. The high frequency of obligate apomixis in Myriopteris and the arid geographical range of the genus make it, arguably, an ideal system for studying the repeated evolution of this trait. Unfortunately, several species complexes within Myriopteris are not entirely resolved. Given these uncertainties, a more in-depth study of the genus is warranted. The primary goals of this undergraduate research project have been (1) to complete a survey of reproductive traits for understudied taxa within Myriopteris, and (2) to perform an in-depth examination of ploidy and reproductive mode for a subset of taxa therein. I have generated new trait data for 15 species within Myriopteris, and I have conducted in-depth trait surveys of four additional species: Myriopteris lanosa (Michx.) Grusz & Windham, Myriopteris rufa Fée, Myriopteris covillei (Maxon) Á. Löve & D. Löve, and Myriopteris clevelandii (D.C. Eaton) Grusz & Windham. For this research conducted from 2022-2023, I have won the Botanical Society of America Undergraduate Research Award.

Myriopteris covillei

Myriopteris covillei is known as a widespread sexual diploid (2n = 2x) that frequently hybridizes with other species in the genus including Myriopteris fendleri (Hook.) E. Fourn., Myriopteris gracillima (D.C. Eaton) J. Sm., and the more distantly related Myriopteris parryi (D.C. Eaton) Grusz & Windham. Within the M. covillei subclade, sexual diploids regularly produce 64 spores per sporangium and, generally, spores have an average diameter of approximately 47.2 µm (Grusz and Windham 2013, Grusz et al. 2014). However, when examining the holotype specimen of M. covillei, Grusz (unpublished) observed a discrepancy between the spore characteristics of the type specimen and our expectations of sexual diploid taxa in this clade. Specifically, the type specimen exhibited spore characteristics consistent with expectations of a sexual tetraploid (2n = 4x). Upon close examination, morphological characteristics of the type specimen—including traits such as abaxial costal scales with ciliate hairs only on the basal lobes and the rhizomal scales strongly appressed with a light brown margin—were intermediate between the widespread sexual diploid previously referred to M. covillei and a second sexual diploid with a partially overlapping species range, Myriopteris clevelandii (D.C. Eaton) Grusz & Windham. Given the frequency of hybridization within Myriopteris, and the overlapping geographical distributions of M. clevelandii and M. covillei, my study delineated the widespread sexual diploid from the hybrid tetraploid type specimen. Four datasets were used: morphological characteristics, spore size and count, geographic data, and molecular data. This research is ongoing, but I have received the American Society of Plant Taxonomy Undergraduate Research Award for my work thus far.

Hornworts & Dioicy

Hornworts (Anthocerotophyta) represent one lineage from the deepest divergence in land plants, and have been relatively understudied, though recent work has added to our knowledge of their diversity and evolution. High quality genomes were recently sequenced for Anthoceros angustus Steph., A. agrestis Paton, and A. punctatus L. (Zhang et al. 2020, Li et al. 2020), however, the genetics responsible for sexual system evolution across the clade have remained a mystery. Villarreal and Renner (2013) found that 40% of hornwort species are dioicous, with many independent events of ancestral monoicous species evolving into dioicous species across the phylogeny. Given the high transition rate from monoicous to dioicous species, and with so little being known about the sex chromosomes of this group, our study attempts to identify the sex chromosomes of three hornwort species. This study generated genomes of male and female individuals from three species (Leiosporoceros dussii (Steph.) Hässel (Leiosporocerotaceae), Phymatoceros phymatodes (M. Howe) R. J. Duff, J. C. Villarreal, Cargill & Renzaglia (Phymatocerotaceae), and Phaeomegaceros chiloensis (Steph.) J.C. Villarreal (Dendrocerotaceae)). The assembled genomes of the three different genera were then used to determine sex chromosome structure and the putative sex of the hornworts themselves. This work was done during the Summer of 2023 as part of an NSF REU under Dr. Fay-Wei Li and Dr. Peter Schafran as part of their larger work assembling a pan-phylum genome of the hornwort clade.
